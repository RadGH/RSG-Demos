Why we’re redoing it

M79 shipped an OpenAI-generated roster that looked great at a glance but broke down under scrutiny. Every pose of every character was generated independently, so nothing held together frame-to-frame.

Helmets appeared and disappeared between idle and attack. Weapons swapped mid-animation. Armor palettes drifted between poses. The Rogue wore three different cloaks depending on which angle you looked at. And then there was the cat.

Cat hall of shame

The Shadow Cat companion spawned with a longsword clamped awkwardly between its paws. A domestic feline, attacking with a two-handed weapon, because the prompt generator didn’t know animals shouldn’t hold steel.
“Filter the prompt by character type so the generator never gives a paw a sword.” — the plan doc, finally

The pipeline

Portrait in, animated set out. Five steps, mostly free operations wrapped around one paid Pixel Engine call per variant (or one bulk /keyframes call per character — whichever costs less on the test run).

1

Source portrait

Existing public/images/portraits/<id>.png. Tactician & Chronomancer regenerated via OpenAI this pass.

2

Pixelate

POST /pixelate — free — normalize to ~256px clean pixel base with a tight 32–48 color palette.

3

Generate variants

POST /animate with the portrait as reference — 6 poses: front idle, east idle, east attack, east spell, east down, portrait.

4

Green-screen key

matte_color: "00ff00" forces a bright green backdrop. ffmpeg colorkey strips it locally.

5

Optimize

Existing optimize_images.sh crushes the final PNGs before the build picks them up.
